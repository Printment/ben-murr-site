# AI Tools Drafts

These drafts are written in a Sanity-ready format so they can be pasted into the CMS and then edited further.

## Codex

### Summary
The tool that has helped me move closest to the build, especially when I want to understand code, structure and the mechanics of shipping something live.

### What it is
Codex is the tool I use when I want AI to work much closer to real files, implementation tasks and the practical steps involved in building and updating a product.

### What I use it for
I use it for website changes, debugging, understanding how pieces fit together and working through build, Git and deployment steps that I would not normally handle myself.

### Where it works well
It works best when the task is concrete and tied to a real environment, especially where the value comes from actually making the change rather than only discussing it.

### Drawbacks or limits
It still benefits from clear direction and some patience. When the context is messy or the goal is vague, it needs more steering than a simpler writing task would.

### Who should use it
Best for people who want to get hands-on with technical work without pretending they already know everything about the tooling.

### My verdict
Codex has been one of the most valuable tools for my own learning because it closes the gap between talking about delivery and actually doing it.

## Sanity

### Summary
The content layer behind this site. It gives me a clean way to edit, structure and grow pages without needing to push code for every content change.

### What it is
Sanity is the headless CMS powering the editable parts of this website, from blog content to the About and AI Tools sections.

### What I use it for
I use it to manage copy, images, ordering and page-level settings so the site can evolve without every change becoming a coding task.

### Where it works well
It works well when content needs structure and flexibility at the same time, especially across multiple page types.

### Drawbacks or limits
The setup is more involved than simple static content. There is a little more schema thinking upfront before it becomes easy to use day to day.

### Who should use it
Useful for people who want a site to stay editable and grow over time without constantly touching code.

### My verdict
Sanity has been worth the setup because it turns the site from a one-off build into something I can keep evolving as a living document.

## Claude

### Summary
A strong tool for thoughtful writing, synthesis and structured reasoning, especially when I want a measured second view on something complex.

### What it is
Claude is an AI assistant I think of as strong on considered writing, careful interpretation and working through material in a calm, structured way.

### What I use it for
I use it when I want to test how clearly something reads, challenge a line of thinking or work through a more reflective problem where pace matters less than quality.

### Where it works well
It works well for longer-form writing, summarising complicated material and helping shape communication that needs to feel deliberate rather than rushed.

### Drawbacks or limits
It can feel a little more restrained and less naturally practical when the task moves closer to direct execution or hands-on implementation.

### Who should use it
Useful for people who spend a lot of time writing, thinking, synthesising and turning complex material into something more digestible.

### My verdict
Claude is one of the tools I would use when I want a thoughtful counterweight to faster-paced workflows and a stronger emphasis on writing quality.

## Gemini

### Summary
A broad AI assistant with strong ecosystem potential, especially where Google tools, search context and general productivity overlap.

### What it is
Gemini is Google’s AI assistant and sits in a useful position between general-purpose support, research help and wider workflow integration.

### What I use it for
I use it to compare answers across models, sense-check thinking and explore how Google’s ecosystem may shape practical AI use in day-to-day work.

### Where it works well
It works well when I want another model perspective or when the task naturally touches research, documents or broader productivity use cases.

### Drawbacks or limits
Its value can depend a lot on where it sits in the wider workflow. On its own, it can feel less distinctive unless you are leaning into the surrounding ecosystem.

### Who should use it
Good for people already working across Google tools or anyone who wants to compare model strengths before deciding where one tool clearly outperforms another.

### My verdict
Gemini is useful to keep in the mix because it adds another serious point of comparison, even if it is not currently the first tool I reach for most often.

## NotebookLM

### Summary
A research and source-grounding tool that is especially useful when I want to interrogate specific documents rather than work from general model knowledge.

### What it is
NotebookLM is best thought of as an AI tool built around source material. Instead of starting from a blank prompt, it works from the documents you give it.

### What I use it for
I use it when I want to understand material more deeply, compare notes, pull out themes or work through a body of information without losing the link back to source content.

### Where it works well
It works well for research, synthesis and anything that benefits from staying grounded in actual reference material rather than a looser general answer.

### Drawbacks or limits
It is less of a general-purpose companion tool. Its strength comes from source-grounded work, so it is less useful if you want broader ideation or more open-ended support.

### Who should use it
Best for people who spend time reviewing documents, reports, notes and source-heavy material and want help making sense of it more quickly.

### My verdict
NotebookLM is one of the more useful specialist tools because it gives a different kind of confidence: not just speed, but stronger grounding in the material you are working from.

## Vercel

### Summary
The hosting and deployment layer that makes this site feel live, fast and easy to update once the foundations are in place.

### What it is
Vercel is the platform I use to host and deploy this site. It takes code from GitHub and turns it into a live production site with minimal friction.

### What I use it for
I use it for deployments, environment variable management, domain connection and making sure changes move from local work into something publicly accessible.

### Where it works well
It works especially well when you want a clean path from code to live site and do not want hosting and deployment to become a project in their own right.

### Drawbacks or limits
It is simple once it is set up, but there is still some learning involved around domains, environment variables and how the deployment flow actually works.

### Who should use it
Useful for people building sites or apps who want hosting to feel modern, fast and closely connected to the development workflow.

### My verdict
Vercel has been one of the most confidence-building tools in this process because it made publishing feel understandable rather than intimidating.

## GitHub

### Summary
The version-control backbone behind the site, giving structure, history and a reliable way to move changes into production.

### What it is
GitHub is where the code for this site lives. It acts as the central source of truth and connects the local build process to deployment.

### What I use it for
I use it to store the project, track changes, commit updates and give Vercel a reliable source to deploy from.

### Where it works well
It works well when you want a clear record of what changed, when it changed and how the live version is being managed over time.

### Drawbacks or limits
If you are new to Git and version control, the terminology and workflow can feel technical at first. It gets easier once the basic rhythm becomes familiar.

### Who should use it
Useful for anyone building digital products who wants a more controlled, transparent and resilient way of managing code.

### My verdict
GitHub has been foundational. It is not the most glamorous tool in the stack, but it is one of the most important because it gives the whole workflow structure.

## Loveable

### Summary
A tool that lowers the barrier to building and exploring product ideas quickly, especially when the goal is speed, experimentation and proof of concept.

### What it is
Loveable is an AI-assisted product-building tool aimed at helping people turn ideas into working outputs more quickly and with less technical friction.

### What I use it for
I use it to explore how far a more guided AI product-building workflow can go, especially when I want to test an idea rapidly without starting from a blank technical setup.

### Where it works well
It works well for fast experimentation, simple prototypes and moments where the main goal is to test whether something is worth pursuing further.

### Drawbacks or limits
The trade-off for speed is usually control. It can be less useful when you want to deeply understand the build itself or make more deliberate structural choices.

### Who should use it
Good for people who want to prototype quickly, validate ideas early and reduce the setup overhead of getting started.

### My verdict
Loveable is valuable when speed matters most, but for me it is strongest as an experimentation tool rather than the place I want to learn the deeper mechanics.

Polsia is part of my broader experimentation with agentic tools and AI-enabled product creation, particularly where the end goal is not just a prototype but a viable business concept.
